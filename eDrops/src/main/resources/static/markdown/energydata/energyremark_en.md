## Comments on the Data

The energy from oil and gas in the North Sea is 16 times greater than our electricity production.
Most of it is exported. If fossil energy is to be replaced by renewables, production must be increased dramatically. 
In practice, we have more than enough problems just replacing our own fossil energy consumption with renewables. 
If we do this too quickly, it will hurt both the population and industry. 
The result may be that industry relocates, we become poorer, 
and global CO₂ emissions increase because production shifts to countries with a higher fossil share.
Real cuts require cooperation across national borders.

Electricity consumption in the EU in 2025 was approximately 2,770 TWh. 
If we add the UK, we get an additional 293 TWh. Norwegian exports hit a record high in 2025 at 34 TWh,
while imports were 11 TWh.

Be aware that the TWh figures are calculated as if we were to produce electricity from the oil and gas, 
whereas the PJ (petajoule) figures refer to the actual amount of energy. Direct comparison is difficult.  

## Analysis

Norway's electricity supply security is reduced and the EU electricity market forces higher average prices upon Norway.
Norway's intention to be Europe's battery is completely ridiculous when comparing Europe's needs against our net exports.
Europe needs 100 times more fossil-free electricity than Norway is currently exporting.

When Germany alone experiences a so-called *Dunkelflaute* (dark period with no wind), 
their demand can exceed 70,000 MW, while wind and solar production drops to almost zero. 
If Norway empties absolutely all of its available export capacity towards Germany and the UK during such an hour, 
we might cover less than 7% of the demand.
Europe must solve the rest themselves anyway, using nuclear power, oil, gas, and coal. 
This includes heavily relying on imports of these resources, including Norwegian gas.  
Paradoxically, Norway risks exporting gas to Germany while having to import electricity in return.

I have nothing against Norway exporting surplus power, but I react
when exports reach levels where water reservoirs risk running low and power shortages become a threat.
This nearly happened in 2022, but heavy rainfall in the autumn prevented a crisis.
This year, in 2026, Norway risks the exact same problem due to the lack of snow in  the mountains for the spring melt.
The Norwegian Government’s massive plans for increased consumption
(data centers, battery factories,  ammonia, hydrogen, electrification)
indicate that the math simply does not add up. 
Meanwhile, existing power-intensive industry must also receive its electricity supply,

**It is the stabilizing effect of hydropower that the EU wants.** 
EU cannot claim that more wind and solar power production in Norway actually helps.
The Norwegian Government's offshore wind adventure is therefore quite wasteful.
EU have recently claifmed that hydropower is not genuinely renewable energy and that it destroys ecosystems.
More wind power makes this even worse due to the necessary hydropeaking (rapid fluctuations in water levels due to balancing power).
The EU is biting its own tail the way it operates.  

What makes this worse is that the planned wind park locations are in the North Sea, right next to other wind installations. 
When the wind blows, too much power is produced all at once across the entire area, causing prices to crash.

## Conclusion

Norway supplies 30% of Europe's gas consumption. The equivalent figure for electricity is 0.76% (Net).

**Norway has the right to limit exports when water reservoirs fall below a certain level,
which is far above what is considered a critical level today according to EEA / Acer.**

Norway's hydropower is no longer cheap due to integration with the EU's power market. 
In addition, we risk an electricity shortage for the exact same reason,
including possible collapse of Norway's own power-intensive industry. 
If we add more wind power, which does not really solve the problem to any significant degree, 
we just destroy more nature. It is NOT worth it. Nuclear power could be the solution, but for the EU, 
this might as well be produced right where it is needed.
